Cap Rate Sensitivity Analysis for Marlborough, Massachusetts

This analysis estimates entry cap rates for single‑family rentals (SF), condos (CC) and small multi‑family (MF) properties using the cleaned MLS dataset (2019–2026) and the rental and expense assumptions defined earlier. Cap rate is calculated as NOI / purchase price, where net operating income (NOI) equals gross potential rent × 95 % occupancy minus operating expenses and property tax. We apply moderate rent assumptions (e.g., ~$2.36 k per month for a one‑bedroom; $2.69 k for a two‑bedroom[1]), a 30 % operating expense ratio for SF and condos, 40 % for multi‑family, and Marlborough’s FY 2025 residential and commercial tax rates (9.86 per $1,000 for SF/CC; 16.96 per $1,000 for MF)[2]. These are entry cap rates based on current rents and list prices; they do not account for value‑add or redevelopment potential.

Marlborough thus offers slightly higher yields than the urban core but not as high as cash‑flow markets in the Midwest.

Observations

  • Single‑family rentals: About 49 % of SF sales in the MLS dataset meet the conservative 5 % cap‑rate threshold, but only about 27 % exceed 6 %. Among currently active listings, very few meet even the conservative threshold, reflecting limited inventory and high pricing.
  • Condos: Condos show a slightly wider spread; 51 % exceed 5 %, and 41 % exceed 6 %. Even under aggressive thresholds, roughly 239 historical condo listings would qualify, including both vintage units needing upgrades and some newer ones.
  • Small multi‑family: Cap rates are depressed; only 79 of 263 MLS multi‑family properties exceed 5.5 %, and fewer than 18 % surpass 6.5 %. None of the current multi‑family listings meet even the conservative threshold. High purchase prices and commercial tax rates suppress yields.

Marlborough on the Income–Appreciation Spectrum

Marlborough is best described as a moderate‑yield appreciation market. It sits between the low‑cap, high‑stability markets of inner‑Boston and the high‑yield, high‑risk markets of the Midwest. Several factors tilt Marlborough toward appreciation:

  • Strong economic drivers: The city hosts major life‑science and high‑tech employers such as Cytiva, Boston Scientific and Sartorius[4]. Median household income ($99,176) and average home value (~$550 k) are high[5], supporting robust demand but also compressing cap rates.
  • Limited developable land and high property values: Marlborough’s location at the junction of I‑495/I‑290/I‑90 fosters commuting and corporate investment, but the supply of single‑family lots is constrained, pushing prices up and yields down. This scarcity mirrors Boston’s limited inventory environment, where Class A and B multifamily cap rates range between 4.8 % and 6.5 %[6].
  • Income stability with moderate cash flow: Renters drawn by life‑science jobs provide stable occupancy, yet the ratio of rent to purchase price limits cash‑on‑cash returns. Multifamily cap rates around 4–5 % indicate reliance on appreciation rather than high income.
  • Macro conditions: Nationwide, multifamily cap rates compressed to 4.92 % for B‑class assets and 5.38 % for C‑class in early 2025[7]. With borrowing costs around 6 %[8] and expenses rising, investors accept lower in‑place yields in exchange for long‑term growth in rents and property values.

Overall, investors in Marlborough should expect moderate current yields and place greater emphasis on value‑add improvements, rent growth and appreciation to achieve desired returns.

Revising Cap‑Rate Bands to Expand Opportunity

The analysis above shows that strict cap‑rate thresholds dramatically limit the pool of candidates—especially for small multi‑family. To broaden the opportunity set while remaining rational, we recommend adjusting cap‑rate bands as follows:

Strategy Original minimum cap rate Revised cap‑rate band Rationale
Core buy‑and‑hold (SF & CC) ≥ 5.5 % 5.0–6.0 % Many solid single‑family and condo assets trade between 5–6 % caps. Lowering the floor to 5 % aligns with Boston Class A/B yields[3] and acknowledges that Marlborough is an appreciation market. It increases eligible properties by ~20 %.
Value‑add / renovation ≥ 5 % entry; ≥ 6.5 % stabilized 4.5–5.5 % entry; ≥ 6.0 % stabilized Allowing slightly lower entry yields (4.5–5 %) captures older condos and SF homes priced above the median but with upside. A 6 % stabilized cap remains realistic given renovation cost and rent growth.
Small multi‑family income ≥ 6 % 5.5–6.5 % Given that Boston Class C multi‑family cap rates range 6.5–7.5 %[3] but Marlborough’s average MF cap is ~4.6 %, a 5.5 % floor is pragmatic. It doubles the number of qualifying properties while still exceeding borrowing costs.
Development / redevelopment ≥ 7 % stabilized 6.5–7.5 % stabilized Large parcels rarely appear; adopting a band rather than a hard minimum acknowledges unique deal structures. Returns should remain at least 150 bp above the going-in cap rate for comparable finished product.
Creative / unconventional (co‑living, ADU) ≥ 6.5 % 6.0–7.0 % Premium rents may justify higher yields, but strict 6.5 % floors eliminate most properties. A 6 % entry with a clear path to ~7 % after ADU creation or co‑living conversion is reasonable given the new Massachusetts ADU law permitting 900‑sq‑ft units by right[9].

Implications: With the revised bands, a larger fraction of MLS properties meet the minimum yield requirements—especially in the core and value‑add categories—while still acknowledging risk and market conditions. Investors must weigh the trade‑off between current cash flow and long‑term appreciation, align cap‑rate targets with available inventory, and perform due diligence to validate rents, expenses and property condition.
