On‑Market Investment Opportunities – Balanced vs Growth Profiles (Marlborough, MA)

This note re‑runs the MLS screening using the balanced and growth‑oriented profiles defined earlier. Balanced rules look for entry cap rates of ~5 % for single‑family/condo deals and ~5.5 % for multifamily, with value‑add candidates priced below the median on a per‑square‑foot basis. Growth rules accept lower initial yields (≈4.5 %) in exchange for upside from renovations, accessory dwelling units (ADUs) or aggressive rent increases. The analysis uses moderate rent assumptions derived from external data sources such as RentCafe and Rent.com[1][2], a 95 % occupancy factor, a 35 % operating expense ratio for value‑add deals and Marlborough’s FY 2025 residential tax rate (0.986 % of purchase price)[3]. Aggressive rent assumptions were used to estimate potential stabilised cap rates for renovation plays.

Only properties with statuses “Active,” “New” or “Under Agreement” were considered. Each opportunity is labelled with the screening profile and the strategy that fits (value‑add/renovation or creative/ADU). Estimated entry and stabilised cap rates are shown using moderate and aggressive rent assumptions respectively. Stabilised cap rates for the creative strategy with an ADU reflect adding a ~900‑sq‑ft unit as allowed under Massachusetts’ new ADU law[4].

Observations

  • Balanced profile: Only 35 High St met the balanced value‑add criteria; it offers a rare combination of high initial yield (>6 %), below‑median price per square foot and straightforward renovation potential. 111 Howe St qualifies under the balanced creative profile due to its strong entry cap and the option to add an ADU; however, this strategy assumes a lower expense ratio and requires permits and construction work.

  • Growth profile: Four additional properties—102 Liberty St, 688 Boston Post Rd E, 30 Broad St and 111 Howe St (with an ADU)—qualify when entry cap thresholds are relaxed. These deals rely on either forced appreciation through renovation (value‑add) or the creation of new rentable space (creative). Rents must be increased to the upper end of the market (e.g., two‑bed apartments at ~$3.3 k/month and three‑bed houses at ~$4.5 k/month)[1] to achieve the pro‑forma caps.

  • Uncertainties: Cap‑rate calculations are based on assumptions about rent levels, operating expenses and tax rates. Actual rents could vary depending on unit condition, neighborhood and regulation; for example, Massachusetts’ proposed rent‑control initiative would cap annual rent increases at CPI or 5 %[7]. No unit‑count data are available for small multi‑family listings, so multifamily opportunities could not be properly evaluated. Buyers should conduct on‑site inspections, verify HOA fees for condos and consult local zoning codes before proceeding.

In conclusion, relaxing cap‑rate thresholds to the balanced and growth profiles increases the number of viable deals in Marlborough. Investors seeking moderate, market‑realistic returns can consider 35 High St or 111 Howe St, while those willing to accept lower initial yields in exchange for significant upside may explore the growth‑oriented properties listed above. Each opportunity’s success will depend on executing renovations, capturing higher rents and managing regulatory and construction risks.
